## Research Interests
Two processes are likely to be universally operative in ecosystems: 1.) the emergence of spatial heterogeneity (either of the abiotic environment or the distribution of organisms) due to ecological interactions, and 2.) the dynamics and outcomes of ecological interactions are structured by spatial heterogeneity at multiple spatial scales. Much of my work is interested in understanding the dynamics and consequences of the feedback between these two interrelated processes and their implications for community assembly and ecosystem functions. 

My theoretical work is often strongly motivated by my field work in coffee agroecosystems, and my field work in coffee agroecosystems is driven by desire to positively contribute to issues of practical significance to society, such as the sustainable management of agroecosystems and the mitigation of biodiversity loss.

## The community ecology of the coffee leaf rust 
<img style="padding: 0 15px; float: right;" src="updated clr banner.png" align="center" width="600">    

<br>
Much of my empirical  research aims to understand **how the assembly of novel ecological communities within agroecosystems impact the dynamics of ecological processes and the subsequent provisioning of ecosystem services such as biological control**, by using the coffee agroecosystem and a widespread and devastating pathogen of coffee, the coffee leaf rust (CLR), *Hemileia vastrix* as a model system. I have worked to elucidate the interaction networks of the coffee rust in Mexico and Puerto Rico in an attempt to understand how the diverse and regionally specific communities of natural enemies in different regions impact the pathogen’s dynamics. *This line of research allows me to couple my passion for natural history and field work with my desire to apply ecological theory to societal issues of practical importance*.
